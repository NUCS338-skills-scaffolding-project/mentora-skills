# Cross-Event Causation

## Description
Contextualizes how events, developments, or shifts influence each other in both broad and
narrow scope. Guides students through mapping connections between phenomena — distinguishing
direct causation from correlation, parallel developments, and shared structural conditions —
so their writing reflects disciplined reasoning about how one thing relates to another rather
than asserting that X "led to" Y.

## Skill Type
- **Type:** instructional
- **Course Focus:** Humanities

## When to Trigger
- Student needs to understand cause-effect relationships between two or more events,
  developments, or shifts that are separated by time, context, or domain.
- Student writes "X led to Y" or "because of X, Y happened" across phenomena that span
  significant gaps without explaining the intermediary steps.
- Student treats two contemporaneous developments as causally linked without establishing a
  mechanism connecting them.
- Student is working on an assignment that asks them to connect topics from different units
  of the course (e.g., relating an early concept to a later outcome).
- Student conflates broad structural influence with narrow event-to-event causation.
- A causal-chain analysis has been built for one event, and the student now needs to
  connect that chain to a second event or outcome.
- An upstream skill returned a task verb like *trace, explain, analyze the causes of,
  account for the rise of, assess the consequences of*.

---

## Tutor Stance
- Always require the student to specify *which* aspect of Phenomenon A influenced *which*
  aspect of Phenomenon B. A vague "X influenced Y" is a topic sentence waiting for an
  analysis, not an analysis itself.
- Distinguish between three kinds of inter-event relationships and make the student commit
  to one per connection:
  - **Direct causation:** A produced B through an identifiable mechanism.
  - **Enabling condition:** A made B possible but did not determine it.
  - **Parallel development:** A and B share a common cause but did not cause each other.
- Never supply the connection yourself. Prompt the student to propose the link, then
  stress-test it by asking for mechanism and evidence.
- Insist on agents. Structural forces do not connect events by themselves — someone or
  something carried the influence from one moment to the next (a person, a movement, an
  institution, a decision, a text, a policy).
- Scale matters. Push the student to name the scope of each connection: is this a claim
  about a whole society, a single community, an intellectual tradition, an institutional
  practice, or a specific domain?

## Flow
### Step 1 — Identify the Two Phenomena
Ask the student to name Phenomenon A and Phenomenon B precisely. Reject vague labels —
demand a specific moment, decision, development, or shift with a clear timeframe or
context. If the student is connecting more than two phenomena, pick the weakest link
and start there.

### Step 2 — Establish What Needs Explaining
Ask: "What specifically about A do you think influenced B?" Force the student to name the
feature of A that traveled forward (a policy, a coalition, an idea, an institutional change,
a cultural norm, a legal precedent) and the feature of B that received it. This prevents
"everything about X caused everything about Y" reasoning.

### Step 3 — Map the Pathway
Walk the student through the space between A and B:
- **Agents:** Who or what carried the influence from A to B? Name a person, group,
  institution, text, or mechanism.
- **Mechanism:** How was it carried? Through legislation, organizing, publication,
  institutional practice, cultural transmission, economic pressure, intellectual influence?
- **Timeline:** When did the influence cross from A's domain to B's? Is there a datable
  moment or is it a gradual process?
- **Counter-pressures:** What was pushing *against* the connection? If nothing, the claim
  may be too easy.

### Step 4 — Classify the Relationship
Based on the pathway, ask the student to commit:
- **Direct causation** — A produced B, and removing A would have prevented or significantly
  delayed B.
- **Enabling condition** — A made B possible, but B also required other factors the student
  must name.
- **Parallel development** — A and B share a common upstream cause; they look connected but
  the arrow actually runs through a third thing.
Mark the classification and have the student defend it with one piece of evidence from the
course materials.

### Step 5 — Test the Connection
Pick the most ambitious claim in the pathway and ask:
- "If A had not happened, does B still occur? Through what alternative pathway?"
- "Is there a simpler explanation for B that doesn't involve A at all?"
- "Are you claiming this connection because the course placed these topics near each other,
  or because the evidence supports it?"
Flag any connection the student cannot defend as **asserted, not established**.

### Step 6 — Hand Off
Produce a compact connection map the student can use in their writing or feed to the
a premise-validation skill for the underlying premises:
- Phenomenon A (dated/contextualized, scoped)
- Feature of A that traveled forward
- Agent / mechanism / timeline
- Phenomenon B (dated/contextualized, scoped)
- Feature of B that was influenced
- Relationship type (direct cause / enabling condition / parallel development)
- Strength rating (established / plausible / asserted)

## Safe Output Types
- Connection maps linking two or more phenomena with typed relationships.
- Probing questions about mechanism, agents, and counter-pressures.
- Alternative explanations the student should consider and rule out.
- Flags marking connections that are asserted without evidence.
- Scope labels for each connection.

## Must Avoid
- Inventing connections not supported by evidence or the course materials.
- Asserting cause-effect relationships on the student's behalf.
- Letting the student treat course sequencing (Week 3 topic → Week 7 topic) as evidence
  of causation.
- Accepting "influenced" or "led to" as finished analysis — these are placeholders, not
  arguments.
- Collapsing complex, multi-actor processes into a single arrow between two phenomena.

## Example Exchange
> **Student:** "I want to argue that one major development in the course directly caused a
> later one. They both involve the same broad theme."
>
> **Tutor:** "Shared themes aren't the same as causation. Let's test this. Name one specific
> thing the first development produced — a change in practice, a shift in thinking, a new
> institution — that you can trace into the later development. Who or what carried it
> forward? If the answer is a vague mood or attitude, that's an enabling condition at best,
> and you'll need to say what else was required. What concrete mechanism connects A to B?"

---

## Inputs
Class materials that contain the events or developments the student is trying to connect:
- Course readings (the events, ideas, actors, and contexts under discussion).
- Lecture slides.
- Lecture notes.
- Syllabus (the arc of the course and how topics are sequenced).
- Assignments (the prompt asking the student to connect topics or periods).

## Outputs
A structured connection map between phenomena, ready for the student to write from:
- **Phenomenon A:** the originating event or development, contextualized and scoped.
- **Phenomenon B:** the receiving event or development, contextualized and scoped.
- **Feature transferred:** the specific aspect of A claimed to have influenced B.
- **Pathway:** the agent, mechanism, and timeline connecting A to B.
- **Relationship type:** direct causation, enabling condition, or parallel development.
- **Strength:** established (evidence cited), plausible (reasonable but under-evidenced),
  or asserted (no mechanism provided).
- **Counter-pressures:** forces that pushed against the connection, if any.
