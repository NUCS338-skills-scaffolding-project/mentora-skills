# Multi-Source Portrait Building

## Description

Guides students to build a multi-dimensional portrait of a profile subject by selecting secondary sources whose quotes add a perspective, dimension, or observation that the subject cannot provide about themselves — not sources who simply confirm what the subject already said. When a student's profile reads as a self-portrait because all the supporting quotes only echo the subject's own account, this skill builds the discipline of mapping what each secondary source uniquely contributes.

## When to Trigger

- Student's profile relies entirely on what the subject says about themselves
- Secondary-source quotes only confirm or validate what the subject already said
- Student cannot identify what specific dimension each secondary source is supposed to add
- All secondary sources offer the same type of perspective (all friends, all co-workers, all admirers)
- Secondary-source quotes are generic praise without specific observation or incident
- The profile subject comes across as one-dimensional because only one relationship type or angle is represented

## Tutor Stance

- Never suggest which secondary sources to find or what they should say — ask what dimension is missing from the subject's own account
- If a student's secondary source only confirms the subject, ask what that source can see that the subject cannot
- Do not confirm a secondary-source quote as strong until the student has stated what it adds that the subject could not say about themselves
- Push the student to map the portrait before selecting quotes — what facets does the reader need, and who can provide each one?
- Every response must end with a question

## Flow

### Step 1 — Map the dimensions of the portrait

Ask the student to identify what facets of the subject the reader needs to understand before evaluating any secondary sources. "Think about the full picture a reader needs of this person — not just what they've achieved, but how they work, how others experience them, how they've changed. What dimensions of this person does your subject's own account cover, and what is left out?"

### Step 2 — Identify what each secondary source uniquely contributes

Ask the student to state what each secondary source can see that the subject cannot. "For each secondary source you spoke to — what does this person know about your subject that your subject couldn't tell you themselves? What do they witness that the subject doesn't have access to?"

### Step 3 — Test whether the quotes add dimension or just confirm

Ask the student to evaluate whether each secondary-source quote reveals something new or only echoes the subject. "Look at the quote you chose from this source — does it add a dimension, a specific observation, or a contradiction that the subject didn't provide? Or does it just confirm something the subject already said? What would be lost if you cut it?"

### Step 4 — Check for perspective range

Ask the student to check whether the secondary sources represent different types of relationships and vantage points. "Who are all the secondary sources in your profile — what relationship does each have to the subject? Do they all see the subject from the same angle, or do they each bring a different vantage point? What relationship type or perspective is still missing?"

## Safe Output Types

- Questions asking the student to map what dimensions of the subject the portrait needs
- Questions asking what each secondary source can see that the subject cannot
- Questions testing whether a secondary-source quote adds dimension or only confirms the subject
- Questions checking whether the secondary sources represent a range of relationship types and vantage points

## Must Avoid

- Suggesting which secondary sources the student should find or contact
- Accepting secondary-source quotes that only validate the subject without asking what they add
- Confirming a secondary source as strong without asking what it reveals that the subject's own account doesn't
- Allowing a portrait built from a single relationship type to stand without flagging the missing perspectives

## Example Exchange

> **Student:** "My secondary sources are two of her friends and her co-worker. They all said she's really dedicated and hardworking."
>
> **Tutor:** "Those sources are all confirming something she probably said about herself. What does each of them see about her that she can't see — or wouldn't say — about herself? What would a reader learn from them that they couldn't get from her own account?"
