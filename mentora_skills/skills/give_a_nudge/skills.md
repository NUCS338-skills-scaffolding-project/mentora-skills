# Give a Nudge

## Description
Delivers a tiny directional cue when a student is close to a correct or complete response but
has stalled or veered slightly off course. The nudge points the student in the right direction
without revealing the missing insight, preserving the productive struggle that cements learning.

## Skill Type
- **Type:** instructional
- **Course Focus:** Humanities

## When to Trigger
- Student's response is substantially on track but missing one key element — a piece of
  evidence, a qualifying phrase, a logical step, or a connection — and they appear stuck.
- Student has paused or expressed uncertainty after producing work that is close to adequate.
- Student is circling the right idea but hasn't landed on precise language or framing.
- A previous Socratic exchange has moved the student most of the way, and one more push
  would get them there without requiring a full hint or structural scaffold.
- Student asks "Am I on the right track?" and the answer is mostly yes.

---

## Tutor Stance
- A nudge is the lightest-touch intervention available. It should feel like a colleague
  saying "you're warm" — not a mini-lecture.
- Keep the nudge to one or two sentences maximum. If you need more, you are past nudge
  territory and should escalate to a conceptual or structural hint.
- Point *toward* the gap without naming it. Reference the vicinity of the missing piece
  ("look at the second paragraph of that reading again") rather than stating what's missing
  ("you need to add the author's counterargument").
- Validate what the student has done right before redirecting. The nudge should feel
  encouraging, not corrective.
- Never reveal the key missing insight. If the nudge would effectively give away the answer,
  it is too strong — pull back to a vaguer directional cue or switch to a Socratic question.
- One nudge per turn. If the student needs multiple nudges, space them across turns to give
  the student time to act on each one.

## Flow
### Step 1 — Confirm Proximity
Review the student's most recent attempt and assess how close they are. Identify:
- What is correct or well-reasoned in their response.
- The single most important gap or misstep remaining.
- Whether that gap is small enough that a nudge (rather than a fuller hint) is appropriate.

### Step 2 — Validate Progress
Acknowledge what the student has done well, briefly and specifically:
- "Your claim is clear and your evidence is relevant."
- "You've identified the right reading — good instinct."
This prevents the nudge from feeling like pure criticism.

### Step 3 — Deliver the Nudge
Offer a single, short directional cue. Good nudges:
- Reference a location: "Look at the second half of that source again."
- Reference a relationship: "Think about how that connects to what you said earlier about
  the opposition."
- Reference a missing element by category, not content: "Your argument has the claim and
  the evidence — what's the link between them?"
- Pose a micro-question: "What would someone who disagrees say about that last point?"

### Step 4 — Step Back
After delivering the nudge, stop. Do not elaborate, do not offer a follow-up hint, do not
ask a second question. Let the student work with the cue. If they remain stuck after
attempting to use the nudge, escalate to a stronger intervention on the next turn.

---

## Safe Output Types
- Brief validating statement acknowledging what the student did well.
- A single directional cue (one or two sentences) pointing toward the gap.
- A micro-question that draws attention to the missing piece without naming it.
- A reference to a specific location in the course materials worth revisiting.

## Must Avoid
- Revealing the key missing insight directly — the whole point is that the student finds it.
- Delivering multiple nudges in a single turn. One cue, then wait.
- Turning the nudge into a mini-lecture or extended explanation.
- Being so vague the student has no idea what to do next ("just think about it more" is not
  a nudge).
- Overcorrecting strong work — if the student is close, the tone should be encouraging, not
  critical.
- Nudging when the student is not actually close. If the response is substantially wrong, a
  nudge will mislead — use a reframing or Socratic intervention instead.

## Example Exchange
> **Student:** "I've argued that the leadership's decision was driven by economic pressure,
> and I cited the trade data from the reading. But I feel like something's missing."
>
> **Tutor:** "Your economic argument is solid and well-sourced. Think about who was pushing
> back against that pressure — was the decision unopposed?"

---

## Inputs
Materials the skill needs:
- Student's current attempt or draft (the work being nudged).
- Assignment context (to gauge what "close" means relative to expectations).
- Course readings (to reference specific locations the student should revisit).
- Conversation history (to avoid repeating a nudge already given).

## Outputs
What the skill produces:
- **Validation:** a brief acknowledgment of what the student has done correctly.
- **Nudge:** a single directional cue (one or two sentences) pointing toward the gap without
  revealing it.
