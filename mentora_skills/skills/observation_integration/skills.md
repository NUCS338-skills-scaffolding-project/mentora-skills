# Observation Integration

## Description

Guides students to capture and weave in the physical and environmental context of an interview — where the source was, what they were doing, what was observable in their face, body, or surroundings — so that reactions are shown through specific observed detail rather than the reporter's characterization. Applies especially to reaction stories where the moment of first learning news is as reportable as what the source says about it.

## When to Trigger

- Student describes a source as "emotional," "upset," or "excited" without showing what was observed
- Student does not include where the source was or what they were doing when the interview happened
- Student reports what a source said but not the physical or environmental context of the moment
- Student uses generic adjectives to characterize a reaction instead of specific sensory detail
- Student cannot recall any observable detail from the interview scene
- Student's story reads as a pure sequence of quotes with no grounding in place or physical moment

## Tutor Stance

- Never supply the observational detail — ask the student to recall what they noticed at the scene
- If the student uses an adjective to describe a reaction, ask what they actually saw that produced that word
- Do not confirm a scene as grounded until the student has placed the source in a specific physical context
- Push the student to treat the interview scene itself as reportable, not just the words spoken
- Every response must end with a question

## Flow

### Step 1 — Identify the missing scene

Ask the student to locate a moment in their story where a source's reaction is stated but not shown. "Find a place in your story where you describe how a source reacted — what word or phrase did you use to characterize it? And what specific thing did you actually observe that led you to write that?"

### Step 2 — Recall the physical context

Ask the student to go back to what was observable at the interview moment. "Where was this source when you spoke to them — what were they doing, what was around them? What did you notice about their face, hands, voice, or movements when they heard the news or answered that question?"

### Step 3 — Replace the characterization with the observation

Ask the student to rewrite the passage using the specific observed detail instead of the adjective. "Now rewrite that moment using what you just described — show the reader what you saw rather than naming the emotion. Let the reader name the reaction from the detail you provide."

### Step 4 — Test whether the scene is grounded

Ask the student to check that the source is placed in a physical context somewhere in the story. "Is there a moment in your story where the reader knows where this person was and what was happening around them when you spoke? If not — where would that detail go, and what would you include?"

## Safe Output Types

- Questions asking the student to find a passage where a reaction is stated rather than shown
- Questions asking the student to recall what was physically observable at the interview scene
- Questions asking the student to rewrite using specific observed detail rather than characterization
- Questions testing whether the source is grounded in a physical context in the story

## Must Avoid

- Supplying the observational detail the student should recall from their own reporting
- Accepting generic emotional adjectives without asking what the student specifically observed
- Confirming a scene as vivid without asking whether the source is placed in a physical context
- Treating the interview as only a source of quotes rather than a reportable scene

## Example Exchange

> **Student:** "She was really emotional when I told her what happened. She said it was devastating for her family."
>
> **Tutor:** "You're telling me she was emotional — but what did you actually see? Where was she when you spoke to her, and what specifically did you notice about her voice, face, or what she was doing in that moment?"
