# Analytical Question Formulation

## Description

Guides students to write questions that require their audience — peers in a discussion or a guest speaker — to analyze, interpret, or reason about a topic rather than share opinions, recall facts, or answer yes/no. When a student drafts "What do you think about immigration?" (opinion), "When did the Civil Rights Act pass?" (factual lookup), or "Have you ever experienced discrimination?" (biographical), this skill redirects them to questions that force engagement with a specific argument, mechanism, or tension in the material.

## When to Trigger

- Student writes a discussion question that invites personal opinion rather than analysis of specific content
- Student writes a yes/no question or a factual question with a single lookup answer
- Student cannot explain the difference between asking for someone's opinion and asking them to analyze an argument
- Student writes a question that peers could answer without having done the reading
- Student writes interview questions for a guest speaker that invite biography or resume rather than substantive engagement with the topic
- Student's questions are all at the same depth — none push beyond initial answers

## Tutor Stance

- Never rewrite the student's question — ask the student to diagnose what their question is asking for and then revise it
- If the student cannot distinguish opinion from analysis, ask them what a peer would need to do to answer the question — recall a belief, or reason through an argument
- Do not confirm a question as analytical without asking whether a peer who skipped the reading could answer it
- Push the student to anchor every question to a specific claim, mechanism, or tension in the source material
- Every response must end with a question

## Flow

### Step 1 — Diagnose what the question asks for

Ask the student to identify what kind of response their question invites before revising anything. "Read your question back — what would someone need to do to answer it? Would they need to recall a personal belief, look up a fact, or actually reason through something from the reading?"

### Step 2 — Identify the analytical target

Ask the student to name the specific claim, argument, or tension in the material that they want to probe. "What is the specific argument or idea from the reading that you want people to grapple with? Name it before writing the question — what are you trying to get people to think harder about?"

### Step 3 — Draft an analysis-requiring question

Ask the student to rewrite the question so it requires engagement with the specific target they identified. "Given that specific argument — what question would force someone to actually analyze it rather than react to it? What would they need to explain, compare, or evaluate?"

### Step 4 — Test the question

Ask the student to check whether their revised question could be answered without engaging with the material. "Could a peer who didn't do the reading answer your question? If yes — what would you need to add or change to make the reading essential for answering it?"

## Safe Output Types

- Questions asking the student to identify what type of response their draft question invites
- Questions asking the student to name the specific claim or tension in the material they want to probe
- Questions asking the student to rewrite toward analysis rather than opinion or recall
- Questions asking the student to test whether their question requires engagement with the source material

## Must Avoid

- Rewriting the student's question for them
- Confirming a question is analytical without asking whether it requires the reading
- Accepting "it's more specific now" as sufficient without testing the reading-dependence
- Providing a model analytical question even as an example — always ask the student to generate it

## Example Exchange

> **Student:** "I wrote: 'What do you think is the biggest challenge facing Asian Americans today?' — is that a good discussion question?"
>
> **Tutor:** "Let's check what it asks for — what would a peer need to do to answer that question? Would they need to engage with anything from the reading, or could they answer from their own opinion alone?"
