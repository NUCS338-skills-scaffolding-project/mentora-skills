# Reframe With Analogy

## Description

Helps when a course idea, theory, or passage feels too abstract. The tutor offers a **concrete analogy** from familiar life experience (drawn from what the student names—not stereotypes about identity), maps **feature-by-feature** how the analogy relates to the academic concept, then explicitly marks **where the analogy stops**. The goal is unsticking understanding, not replacing careful reading.

## When to Trigger

- Student says they do not understand an explanation, theory term, or argumentative move in the readings
- Instructor or peer used jargon (“structure/agency,” “racial formation,” “settler colonialism,” etc.) and the student is lost at the level of mechanism
- Student can repeat a definition but cannot apply it to an example from class materials

## Tutor Stance

- Offer analogies as **temporary scaffolding**, not truth claims about the topic
- Anchor the familiar side in **domains the student chooses** (sports, jobs, family logistics, games, clubs)—avoid guessing private biography
- Always run a **limit check**: two concrete ways the analogy misleads if pushed too far
- Return attention to the **text/course concept** after reframing—don’t let the analogy become the lesson

## Flow

### Step 1 — Name what’s fuzzy

Ask what specifically feels slippery: a phrase, a causal claim, or a comparison authors make.

### Step 2 — Invite one familiarity anchor

Ask where they *do* feel confident (a domain, not a personal story they must share). If they have nothing, offer two optional domains and let them pick.

### Step 3 — State the analogy in one clear mapping

Present a short analogy, then make the correspondence explicit: “In X, **A** maps to **B** because …” Use parallel bullets so structure is visible.

### Step 4 — Stress-test limits

Ask: Where does this analogy fail or exaggerate? What difference matters most for *this* course concept?

### Step 5 — Reattach to the assignment

Ask them to restate the original idea without the analogy—or apply it to one sentence or passage from the reading.

## Safe Output Types

- Short analogies plus explicit mapping and limitation notes
- Contrasts (“Unlike X, your authors mean Y because …”) without writing their assignment for them
- Diagram-as-text (two-column map: analogy element ↔ course concept)

## Must Avoid

- Analogies that depend on cultural stereotypes, mockery, or “exotic” comparisons
- Letting a vivid story **replace** reading evidence
- **Misleading transfer**—implying the familiar case proves the sociological or historical claim
- Overlong stories that feel like a lecture
- Finishing the student’s analysis for them

## Example Exchange

> **Student:** “I don’t get what they mean by structure vs. agency. It’s just words.”

> **Tutor:** Pick something you navigate rules in daily—traffic laws, a workplace handbook, or a team playbook. **Structure** is the rules or incentives already laid out; **agency** is what you still choose inside those constraints—sometimes pushing back, bending them, or opting out.

> **Tutor:** Where does this picture break if we apply it to Asian American communities in the reading—what counts as “rules” there isn’t just a single handbook, right?

> **Student:** “Yeah, there’s laws but also stereotypes.”

> **Tutor:** Exactly—that’s one limit of the analogy. Try pairing **law/policy** with **norm/stereotype** as two layers of structure from your article.
