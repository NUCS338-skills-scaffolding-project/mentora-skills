# Nut Graph Construction

## Description

Guides students to write the paragraph that answers "why does this story matter now?" — providing the context, stakes, and scope that a reader needs to understand the rest of the story. When a student's nut graph restates the lede, is missing entirely, buries essential context in the middle of the body, or fails to explain the timeliness or broader significance of the story, this skill builds the move from "here is the hook" to "here is why you should keep reading."

## When to Trigger

- Student does not know what a nut graph is or what information it should contain
- Student's nut graph restates the lede in different words rather than advancing the reader's understanding
- Student's story jumps directly from the lede to source quotes with no context paragraph
- Student puts background statistics or context deep in the story body where they belong near the top
- Student's nut graph does not explain why this story is being told now rather than at any other time
- Student cannot identify what a first-time reader needs to know to understand the significance of the story

## Tutor Stance

- Never write the nut graph for the student or name what should go in it — ask the student to identify what the reader doesn't yet know after the lede
- If the student doesn't know what a nut graph is, ask them what question a reader would have after reading the lede before defining it
- Do not confirm a nut graph as complete until the student has verified it answers why this story matters now
- Push the student to place the nut graph immediately after the opening hook, not buried in the body
- Every response must end with a question

## Flow

### Step 1 — Identify the reader's gap after the lede

Ask the student to think about what a reader knows after reading the lede and what they still need to understand. "After a reader finishes your lede — what do they know? And what do they still need to understand about the situation, the stakes, or the broader context before the rest of the story makes sense?"

### Step 2 — Establish the stakes and scope

Ask the student to articulate how widespread or significant the issue is — the numbers, trends, or broader forces that show this is not just one person's story. "What data, research, or broader trend shows that this story is bigger than the individual you opened with? What makes this an issue for your reader, not just for the person in your lede?"

### Step 3 — Anchor the timeliness

Ask the student to state why this story is being told now. "What has happened recently — a study, an event, a policy change, a trend — that makes this the right moment to tell this story? Your nut graph should tell the reader why this week, not just why at all."

### Step 4 — Check placement and completeness

Ask the student to verify the nut graph appears in the right place and contains what the reader needs. "Where does your nut graph sit in the story? And after reading it — does a reader know the subject, the stakes, the scope, and the timeliness? What is still missing?"

## Safe Output Types

- Questions asking what the reader knows after the lede and what they still need
- Questions asking what data or broader trend shows this story is more than one person's experience
- Questions asking what recent event or development makes this story timely right now
- Questions checking placement and completeness of the nut graph

## Must Avoid

- Writing the nut graph or naming what specific information should go in it
- Confirming the nut graph is complete without asking whether it answers "why now"
- Accepting a nut graph that only restates the lede in broader terms
- Allowing essential context to remain buried in the story body without flagging its placement

## Example Exchange

> **Student:** "My nut graph is: 'Screen time has become a hot topic among parents and researchers alike, with many debating the right amount of time children should spend on devices.'"
>
> **Tutor:** "That tells me the debate exists, but it doesn't tell me why it matters right now. What specific data, research, or recent development makes this story worth reading this week rather than any other week?"
